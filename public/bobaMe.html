Terminology

Here is a list of terms and phrases and their definitions I use to describe the boba franchises:

  • classic teahouse a franchise specializing in tea-based drinks. They are most likely to lack artificial flavorings and "Western" flavors, including fruits and berries such as coconut and strawberry, as well as "Western" drink textures such as smoothies.
  • bubble tea a beverage that comes with tapioca pearls as a set (non-removable).
  • drinks customizable or specialized (unique to franchise) beverages.
  • drink customization mainly refers to the ability to adjust sugar and ice levels, but can also include the availability of drink size options and topping selections.
  • fusion counterpart to classic teahouse. A fusion franchise usually has an overwhelmingly large amount of menu options in order to cater to a wider range of taste palettes. Most likely includes "Western" flavors and drink textures.
  • tea describes a beverage using freshly brewed tea from leaves, rather than a powder base.
  • traditional flavors can refer to classic teahouse flavors such as black, green, or oolong tea, but can refer to a "deeper level" of tradition in the case of franchises offering traditional desserts (flavors such as purple rice, sweet potato, mung bean, etc.).

Boba is not so much an obsession or addiction than it is a lifestyle for me. It may sound crazy, and yes, nowadays there are many who obsess over bubble tea too â€” it's not just me. However, I think I can set myself apart from the rest, as it is a lifestyle. Boba is often the highlight of a day for me; something about it actually makes me happy. For example, when I go on trips, the most exciting thing to me is visiting boba locations that are new to me and aren't available where I live.

Why Japan?

I've been studying the Japanese language for six years and have visited the country a couple times. The tapioca craze has recently gone on steriods there, just in time for my most recent trip. Japan is also the only "real" foreign country *sorry Canada* I've travelled to and therefore have been able to discover boba-wise.

In particular, my most recent trip to Japan was the first I actively went boba-hunting (an actual newly-made vocabulary word in Japanese) in the country. Prior to this trip, I loved the drink for its sweetness in particular, always refusing to order less than 100% sugar. However, because my favorite flavor is matcha (powder green tea), and Japan is the home of it, I've come to realize the true flavors of the "tea" in "bubble tea." Now, I can't stand anything over 30% sugar!
